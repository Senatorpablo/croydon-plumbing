Bathroom Renovation Tips

Published 5 March 2025 · 10 min read

Introduction

A bathroom renovation is one of the most impactful improvements you can make to your home. It's the room you use every single day, and its condition directly affects your quality of life and your property's value. Yet bathroom renovations are also among the most complex home improvement projects — combining plumbing, electrics, tiling, ventilation and waterproofing in a compact space. Get any of these wrong and you'll be dealing with leaks, mould and costly repairs for years. This guide walks Croydon homeowners through every stage of a successful bathroom renovation, from initial planning to final finishing touches.

Budgeting Realistically

The average bathroom renovation in the Croydon area costs between £4,000 and £10,000, depending on the size of the room, the quality of fixtures and whether you're changing the layout. A basic refresh — new suite, tiles and paint without moving any plumbing — starts around £3,000–£4,000. A mid-range renovation with better fixtures, underfloor heating and a layout change runs £6,000–£8,000. A high-end renovation with premium materials, custom cabinetry and luxury features can exceed £12,000.

Always budget an extra 15–20% for unexpected issues. Once you start ripping out an old bathroom, you may discover damaged floorboards, corroded pipes, inadequate waterproofing or outdated electrical wiring that must be brought up to current building regulations. These discoveries are extremely common in Croydon's older Victorian and Edwardian properties.

Design and Planning

Good bathroom design starts with how you use the space. Consider who uses the bathroom and when. A family bathroom needs durability and storage, while an en-suite prioritises compact efficiency. Think about the flow: can two people use the room simultaneously? Is the toilet positioned for privacy even with the door open? Can the shower be used without soaking the entire floor?

Keep existing pipe locations in mind — moving plumbing waste pipes is expensive and sometimes impossible depending on the fall required. Where possible, position new fixtures close to existing waste points to minimise cost and complexity. A skilled plumber can advise on what's feasible within your property's structure. Choosing Fixtures

Your choice of sanitaryware defines the bathroom's style and practicality. For smaller Croydon bathrooms, wall-hung toilets and basins free up floor space and create a cleaner look. Close-coupled toilets remain the most popular for their simplicity and easy maintenance. When selecting a bath, consider how you actually use it — a long soak demands depth, while a shower-over-bath setup works for busy households.

For showers, a thermostatic mixer valve is worth the extra cost. It maintains a constant temperature even when someone runs a tap elsewhere, preventing scalding — especially important for families with young children. Walk-in showers with frameless glass panels offer an open, modern look and eliminate the cleaning hassle of shower doors. If your water pressure is low — common in older Croydon properties — choose a shower designed for low-pressure systems or consider a pumped shower.

Tiling and Flooring

Tiles are the most popular bathroom wall and floor covering for good reason: they're waterproof, durable and available in virtually any style. Porcelain tiles are the best all-round choice — they're denser than ceramic, less porous, more resistant to stains and available in formats that mimic stone, wood and concrete. Large-format tiles (600mm × 300mm or bigger) reduce grout lines, making the room feel bigger and cleaning easier.

For floors, ensure tiles have a slip-resistant rating (R10 or higher for bathrooms). Underfloor heating is a worthwhile addition — electric mat systems cost £300–£600 for a typical bathroom and make cold tile floors comfortably warm. They're also energy-efficient, typically adding less than £2 per week to your electricity bill during winter.

Lighting and Ventilation

Good lighting transforms a bathroom. Layer your lighting: a central ceiling light for general illumination, task lighting around the mirror (ideally side-mounted to avoid shadows on your face), and accent lighting for atmosphere. LED lighting is now standard — it's energy-efficient, long-lasting and available in warm tones that flatter skin tones. If you want a luxury touch, IP65-rated LED downlights above the shower or bath create a spa-like feel.

Ventilation is legally required in any bathroom without a window and practically essential in all of them. An extractor fan should achieve at least 15 litres per second extraction rate for a standard bathroom. Choose a humidistat-controlled fan that automatically switches on when moisture levels rise — many Croydon bathrooms suffer from mould because occupants forget to use the manual switch. Duct the fan to an external wall, not into the loft space where moisture creates condensation and rot.

Waterproofing

Waterproofing — or tanking — is the most critical unseen element of any bathroom renovation. Tile grout is not waterproof; it's water-resistant at best. Water that penetrates grout seeps into the substrate and eventually damages walls and floors. Proper waterproofing involves applying a liquid membrane or tile-backer board to shower areas and behind baths before tiling. In wet rooms, the entire floor and lower walls must be tanked.

Don't cut corners on waterproofing. The £200–£400 it adds to your renovation cost is trivial compared to the £5,000+ you'll spend repairing water damage to floors, ceilings and structural timbers if your shower leaks. Storage Solutions

Insufficient storage is the most common complaint about bathrooms. Plan storage early in the design process, not as an afterthought. Recessed shelves in shower niches hold shampoo and soap without protruding into the shower space. Wall-mounted cabinets above the toilet use dead space. Vanity units with drawers under the basin hide cleaning products and toiletries. Tall, narrow linen cabinets fit alongside baths or in unused corners.

If your bathroom is small, consider storage solutions that maximise every centimetre: mirrored cabinets that combine reflection and storage, corner shelving units, and hook rails instead of towel bars. Custom-built storage costs more but is tailored precisely to your space and needs.

Hiring the Right Contractor

A bathroom renovation involves multiple trades — plumbing, tiling, electrics, plastering, decoration — and coordinating them is a project in itself. The simplest approach is to hire a single contractor who manages the entire project. You'll pay a premium (typically 15–20% on top of individual trade costs) but gain a single point of accountability and a guaranteed completion date. Alternatively, you can project-manage yourself and hire individual trades, but this requires significant time, knowledge, and the ability to sequence work correctly.

Always verify that your plumber is Gas Safe registered (essential if gas work is involved), that your electrician is NICEIC or NAPIT approved, and that your tiler can show you examples of previous bathroom work. Check reviews, ask for references, and ensure you have a written contract that specifies the scope of work, payment schedule, completion date and warranty terms. Realistic Timeline

A typical bathroom renovation takes 2–3 weeks from strip-out to completion. The first 2–3 days involve stripping the old bathroom and any necessary structural work. Plumbing first-fix takes 1–2 days. Electrical first-fix 1 day. Waterproofing and curing 1–2 days. Tiling is the longest phase at 4–6 days including grouting. Second-fix plumbing and electrics 1 day. Decorating and finishing 1–2 days. Finally, silicone sealing needs 24 hours to cure properly before the bathroom can be used.

Delays are common — waiting for tile deliveries, discovering hidden problems, or simply because good tradespeople are in demand. Build at least a week's contingency into your plans, especially if you only have one bathroom in the house.
